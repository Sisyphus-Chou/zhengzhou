Fortress of the Fearful: Identity Anxiety and Institutional Security-Seeking in Taiwan
This dissertation examines how identity anxiety—understood as ontological (in)security—reshapes citizens’ trust in Taiwan’s military and civilian institutions under sustained external threat. Building theory at the intersection of political psychology and institutional trust, I develop the Anxiety Absorption Threshold Mechanism (AATM): when oversight and integrity are weak, anxiety spills into defensive attachment; when they are strong, anxiety is absorbed and converted into integrative, institution-based trust. Using Asian Barometer Survey Taiwan Waves 3–6 (2009–2023), the study tests non-linear thresholds and cross-wave dynamics to map how fear is redistributed across institutions in a threatened democracy.
Project Overview
  • Theoretical Framework: Ontological Security, Identity Anxiety, Institutional Trust, Threat Perception
  • Research Design: Hierarchical models; generalized additive models (GAM) for non-linear thresholds; mediation & interaction tests; Latent Class Analysis for “security-seekers” vs “institutional integrators”
  • Data: Asian Barometer Survey (Taiwan), Waves 3–6, 2009–2023
  • Status: Master’s Dissertation, Sciences Po Paris (2026)
  • Advisors: Kevin Arceneaux
Key Findings
  • Negative baseline: Identity anxiety generally depresses trust in the military; effects are strongest among politically aligned groups (a “vulnerability within alignment” pattern).
  • Threat is non-linear: mid-level threat can buffer anxiety’s corrosive effects, but extreme threat produces a rebound that erodes trust again.
  • Oversight as absorber: strong oversight and perceived integrity flip the slope—evidence of an Anxiety × Oversight × Threat interaction consistent with AATM.
  • Redistribution, not monotone change: trust shifts across institutions; LCA shows movement from security-seekers toward institutional integrators as oversight increases.
