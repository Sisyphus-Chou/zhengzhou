Fortress of the Fearful: Identity Anxiety and Institutional Security-Seeking in Taiwan
This dissertation examines how identity anxiety and perceived ontological insecurity shape citizens’ trust redistribution across military and civilian institutions in democratic Taiwan. Drawing on theories of ontological security, threat perception, and institutional trust, the project conceptualizes identity anxiety as a defensive psychological state that emerges under perceived external threat and social exclusion. Using large-scale survey data from the Asian Barometer Survey (Waves 3–6), it investigates when and how anxiety reinforces or erodes institutional confidence, especially amid intensifying cross-Strait tensions.

The study develops the Anxiety Absorption Threshold Mechanism (AATM) to explain how political oversight and institutional integrity determine whether anxiety is absorbed into constructive trust or converted into defensive distrust. This mechanism provides a novel framework for understanding how democracies under geopolitical duress transform fear into institutional resilience or vulnerability.
Project Overview
  • Theoretical Framework: Ontological Security, Identity Anxiety, Institutional Trust, Threat Perception
  • Research Design: Hierarchical modeling, generalized additive models (GAM), mediation and interaction analysis, non-linear threshold modeling
  • Data: Asian Barometer Survey Taiwan (Waves 3–6), 2009–2023
  • Status: Master’s Dissertation, Sciences Po Paris (2026)
  • Advisors: Kevin Arceneaux
Key Findings
  • Identity anxiety significantly decreases military trust but exhibits non-linear reversals under heightened oversight and integrity conditions.
  • Oversight and perceived institutional fairness act as absorbers of anxiety, converting defensive attitudes into integrative trust.
  • Evidence supports a redistributive pattern of trust: anxiety shifts confidence between military and civilian institutions rather than uniformly eroding it.
  • The Anxiety Absorption Threshold Mechanism (AATM) illuminates how democracies manage psychological insecurity under sustained external threat.
